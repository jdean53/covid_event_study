Risk Factor Analysis for Equity Returns During Covid¶

Goal: The Goal of this anlysis is to show optimal firm structure ahead of the COVID-19 crises. Specifically, I analyze firm Financing, Operating, and Investing activity prior to the crises and compare these measures to equity returns during the crisis. This will show which firms were best and worst positioned

Methodology: I gather data from firms in the S&P 500. Specifically, I analyze Firm's 10-Ks using basic Natural Language Processing to search for key phrases and terms that would indicate certain risk factors. I then look at firm accounting data and its relation with equity returns. The combination of these measures should indicate the optimal stucture.

Data: 10-K's are collected from SEC Edgar. Firm accounting data is provided. The complete analysis considers 352 firms across various industries and subsectors

Conclusions: Companies with less debt in their Capital Structure and more effort put into New Product Development and R&D Tended to outperform during COVID

Top Down Data Analysis¶

Text Risk Ractors: Text risk factors measures mentions of phrases associated with the following ideas. I group the types of these similar to a firm's Cash Flow statement (financing, operating, and investing), as an event like COVID should kill firm's Cash Flows. I then look at the correlation of these risk measures against returns week over week of 2020 (week 10 being the first week of March). The relationship between these should then show optimal firm structure leading into the crisis. I define the words to be "close" if they are within 30 words of each other.

  • Financing: Interest Rate Risk - Measures risks associated with interest rate moves, looks for the words 'rate' and 'risk' to be close to each other.
  • Financing: Credit Risk - Measures risks associated with debt, looks for the words 'debt' and 'risk' to be close to each other.
  • Financing: Credit Capacity - Measures mentions of firms drawing down lines of credit, looks for the words 'credit facility' and 'draw' to be close to each other (i.e. looking for the phrase "draw down on the credit facility").
  • Operating: Product Risk - Measures firms mentioning new products, looks for the words 'new' and 'product' to be close to each other
  • Operating: Wage Risk - Measures firms mentioning increasing wages, looks for the words 'increase' and 'wage' to be close to each other.
  • Operating: Maintenance - Measures firms mentioning maintenance costs, looks for the words 'maintenance' and 'cost' to be close to each other.
  • Investing: CAPEX - Measures firms mentioning growth CAPEX, looks for the words 'capital', 'expenditures', and 'growth' to be close to each other (i.e. measuring firms spending money to grow).

Accounting Risk Factors: Accounting measures to proxy similar activities to the text risk factors. Financing shows firm leverage, operating shows size, and investing shows money put into future activities.

  • Financing: Total Debt Ratio - Debt to Assets
  • Financing: Total Debt to Market - Debt to Market Value
  • Operating: Log of Employee Count - Measures firm employees
  • Investing: R&D Ratio - R&D to Assets

Drawing Conclusions¶

Events

  • Crisis Week - Week 11 was 3/9/20 to 3/15/20
  • Other weeks included around the crisis data

Risk Factor Inter-Relations

  • As expected, Credit Risk is correlated with Total Debt
  • As expected, Product Risk is correlated with R&D
  • Unexpected, Product Risk is correlated with Total Debt, but not Credit Risk
  • Unexpected, R&D is Correlated with Total Debt

Risk Factor Return Relations

  • Total Debt to Market Value vs Returns was consistently the largest correlation
  • Product Risk and R&D Similarly correlated to Returns as well
  • Weak Credit Risk Correlation for the smaller time period

Graphic Relationships

  • Week 11 Returns and Debt-to-Market-Value have a negative relationship - albeit with a large spread. Implies more debt in the cap-structure led to weaker returns, but not a strong enough relationship to be predictive on its own.
  • Week 10 Returns and Product Risk have a weakly positive relation. Implies a firm diversifying its offering was likely to outperform others.
  • Week 10 Returns and Research and Development act similarly to product risk. Confirms as investors came to realization about COVID, they looked to put their money into more companies with more diversified product offering.
  • Week 14 Returns and Reasearch and Development are very strongly correlated and their relationship is tight.
